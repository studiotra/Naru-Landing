This article examines what the latest CCUS news from Korea, Canada, and the broader international market signals for bilateral collaboration, and what industry leaders and policymakers should be watching over the next 24 months.

## The Global CCUS Crossroads

The numbers tell a story of momentum constrained by scale. Global operational CO₂ capture capacity has grown to roughly 50 million tonnes per year as of early 2026, backed by a pipeline of more than 600 projects and investment that has tripled in recent years to approximately $6.4 billion. Yet climate-aligned pathways still require roughly 1 billion tonnes of annual capture capacity by mid-century.

What is changing, according to analysts at S&P Global, Wood Mackenzie, and the IEA, is the structural character of CCUS development. The sector is moving from government-funded demonstration projects toward commercial investment decisions.

> CCUS has reached a turning point. It is now firmly positioned as a critical tool for meeting global climate goals. The industry's next challenge is to scale up, deploying more projects and deploying them faster.
>
> — Jamie Burrows, DNV

## South Korea: CCUS as a Legal and Strategic Imperative

South Korea's CCUS ambitions in 2026 are no longer driven by aspiration alone. They are driven by law. Korea's Phase 4 Emissions Trading Scheme (2026–2030) now formally integrates CCUS reductions into carbon accounting.

Korea's industrial base is concentrated in coastal zones rich in emissions but poor in proximate geological storage. That is not a technical failure — it is a structural opening for cross-border storage partnerships.

## Canada: Infrastructure, Scale, and a $200 Billion Window

Canada's clean-energy position in 2026 has been reshaped by the National Electricity Strategy, which aims to double grid capacity by 2050. Alberta sits at the center of Canada's CCUS story with deep saline aquifer storage capacity and mature carbon-storage regulation.

## Where Korea and Canada Converge

The strategic logic of Korea-Canada CCUS collaboration rests on four complementary asymmetries:

- **Korea has industrial CO₂ volume and limited domestic storage; Canada has geological storage capacity.**
- **Korea has engineering depth in industrial process integration; Canada has world-class project development and regulatory infrastructure.**
- **Korea needs credible CCUS pathways for K-ETS compliance; Canada needs industrial partners to justify infrastructure investment at scale.**
- **Both countries can shape bilateral norms while US policy direction remains less reliable.**

## Policy and Investment Implications

1. **Formalize a bilateral CCUS cooperation framework** with clear accounting and transport rules.
2. **Explore how K-ETS could interface with Canadian carbon pricing and storage pathways.**
3. **Prioritize Alberta–Korea industrial pilots** in hard-to-abate sectors.
4. **Engage Indigenous and community stakeholders early** in Canadian project design.

## Conclusion: The Window Is Now

The CCUS signals of 2026 are not just a status update. They are a structural market signal. The bilateral institutional infrastructure for Korea-Canada CCUS collaboration is still incomplete — and that is precisely why the next 24 months matter.
